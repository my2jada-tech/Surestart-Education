What is a Food Chain? üçÉ‚û°Ô∏èüê∞‚û°Ô∏èü¶ä

A food chain shows how energy and nutrients move from one living thing to another. It starts with producers (plants) and continues through different levels of consumers.

Producer: An organism that makes its own food using sunlight through a process called photosynthesis. Plants are producers!
Examples: Trees, grass, flowers, algae, seaweed

Types of Consumers:

1. Primary Consumer (Herbivore): Animals that eat only plants. They are plant-eaters!
Examples: Rabbits, cows, grasshoppers, deer, caterpillars
Remember: "Primary" means first - these are the FIRST animals to eat in the food chain!
2. Secondary Consumer: Animals that eat primary consumers. They can be carnivores (meat-eaters) or omnivores (eat both plants and animals).
Carnivore Examples: Snakes, frogs, birds, foxes
Omnivore Examples: Bears, raccoons, chickens
Remember: "Secondary" means second - these are the SECOND level of eaters!
3. Tertiary Consumer (Top Predator): Animals that eat secondary consumers. They are often the top predators in their environment.
Examples: Eagles, sharks, lions, owls, killer whales
Remember: "Tertiary" means third - these are usually at the TOP of the food chain!
Decomposer: Organisms that break down dead plants and animals and return nutrients to the soil.
Examples: Fungi (mushrooms), bacteria, earthworms
Important: Decomposers are nature's recyclers! They help complete the cycle.

Energy Flow in Food Chains ‚ö°

Important Rule: Energy DECREASES as you move up the food chain!

This happens because animals use energy for:

  • Moving around
  • Staying warm
  • Growing
  • Breathing

Only about 10% of energy passes from one level to the next!

Arrows in Food Chains ‚û°Ô∏è

The arrows show the direction of energy flow!

The arrow points FROM what gets eaten TO what eats it.

Example: Grass ‚û°Ô∏è Rabbit ‚û°Ô∏è Fox

This means: Energy flows from grass to rabbit to fox

In the Caribbean (Turks and Caicos Islands), food chains include unique animals like:
  • Rock iguanas (primary consumers - eat plants)
  • Flamingos (eat small shrimp and algae)
  • Hawks (tertiary consumers - hunt smaller birds and lizards)
  • Barracudas (top predators in the ocean)

üìö Study Guide: The Excretory System

What is the Excretory System? ü´ò

The excretory system is a group of organs that work together to remove waste materials from your body. Your body produces waste every day through digestion, respiration, and other life processes. If these wastes aren't removed, they can become toxic (poisonous) and harm your body.

Main Functions:

  1. Removes waste products like urea, excess salts, and toxins
  2. Regulates water balance to prevent dehydration or water overload
  3. Maintains chemical balance (homeostasis) in the body
  4. Controls blood pressure by managing the amount of water in the blood

Major Organs and Their Roles:

1. KIDNEYS ü´ò
  • Bean-shaped organs located at the back of your abdomen
  • You have TWO kidneys (left and right)
  • Each kidney contains about 1 MILLION tiny filtering units called nephrons
  • Nephrons filter waste, excess water, and salts from your blood
  • The clean blood goes back into circulation, and waste becomes urine
2. URETERS
  • Two thin tubes (one from each kidney)
  • Carry urine from the kidneys to the bladder
  • Use wave-like movements called peristalsis to push urine along
3. BLADDER
  • A muscular storage sac that holds urine
  • Expands as it fills up with urine
  • Sends signals to your brain when it's time to urinate
  • Can hold about 400-600 mL of urine (about 2 cups!)
4. URETHRA
  • A tube that carries urine from the bladder to outside the body
  • In males, it also carries semen during reproduction
  • Has a sphincter (muscle) that controls when urine exits

Other Organs That Help Remove Waste:

Organ Type of Waste Removed How It Helps
Lungs Carbon dioxide and water vapor Breathing out (exhalation)
Skin Sweat (water, salt, and urea) Sweat glands during perspiration
Liver Bile and toxins Breaks down old red blood cells and poisons
Large Intestine Solid waste (feces) Removes undigested food and bacteria

Process of Urine Formation:

  1. FILTRATION: Kidneys filter waste from the blood
  2. REABSORPTION: Useful substances (like water and glucose) return to the blood
  3. EXCRETION: The remaining waste becomes urine and is passed out of the body

Keeping Your Excretory System Healthy:

Drink 6-8 glasses of water daily - Water helps your kidneys flush out waste!
Eat fruits and vegetables - They contain water and fiber to help your system work properly
Avoid too much salt, sugar, and processed foods - These make your kidneys work harder
Practice good hygiene - This prevents urinary tract infections (UTIs)
Don't hold urine for long periods - Go when you need to!
Exercise regularly - This improves circulation and body function

Common Excretory System Problems:

  • Urinary Tract Infection (UTI): Caused by bacteria entering the urinary tract
  • Kidney Stones: Hard lumps formed from minerals and salts in the kidneys
  • Kidney Failure: When kidneys cannot filter waste properly
  • Dehydration: When there's not enough water in the body for normal function

Amazing Facts! üéØ

Each kidney has over 1 MILLION nephrons! That's like having a million tiny filters!
The average adult produces about 1.5 liters of urine per day - that's about 3 water bottles!
Your right kidney is slightly LOWER than your left kidney because of the liver above it!
The excretory system works CONTINUOUSLY, even while you sleep! It never takes a break!
